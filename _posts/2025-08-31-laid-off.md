This semester was interesting because this was the semester I got laid off from my job at GE Vernova. Getting laid off is never easy, especially during a time when I needed some form of stability. My siblings were graduating, I was getting engaged, and I was taking a round trip to Dubai, so there were going to be some expenses left and right. I was given around 2 months between the time of being told I was being laid off and the time of actually being jobless, so I thought it best to enroll in two classes to keep me busy.  I travelled to Dubai just before my classes started and that's also when I started to apply to jobs and study Leetcode again😒 (What a miserable website).

Reinforcement Learning was taught by 2 professors, Dr. Scott Niekum and Dr. Peter Stone. The concept of a class being taught by 2 professors isn't unfamiliar to me, but usually classes taught by 2 professors are split into two halves where each professor would focus on teaching their respective half. This class however would alternate sections between the professors, which isn't anything special. What was actually unorthodox was the teaching method itself. The general expectation was that students would read a chapter of the book [Reinforcement Learning: An Introduction](https://web.stanford.edu/class/psych209/Readings/SuttonBartoIPRLBook2ndEd.pdf) and then use the lectures to supplement the understanding that you have of the class.

I am personally not a book reader, so I actually struggled with this new teaching method. The book itself was really good and both of the professors actually studied under the purview of Dr. Andrew Barto, one of the authors of the book. But I did not enjoy the process of studying for the class. We were also asked to write up a few questions or observations to demonstrate that we read the chapters, which was probably the most draining part of studying for the course. The assignments and quizzes themselves weren't too bad, especially if you go through the algorithm slowly and really internalize what its doing and compare it to other algorithms.

Advanced Predictive Models was obviously the easier of the two classes. It was also taught by 2 professors, Dr. Catherine (Kate) Calder and Dr. Purna Sarkar. Dr. Calder taught the first half and Dr. Sarkar taught the second. This was more of a math heavy course and covered many different statistical concepts that I had not really covered before such as Time Series, Spatial Statistics, Network Models. PCA/SVD was also covered, but that was more of a recap of what I studied in the Machine Learning class. Assignments and Quizzes were also easy enough - just study the lectures and you'll be fine.
