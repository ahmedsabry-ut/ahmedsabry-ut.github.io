After being humbled by the first semester, I realized I needed to ease off a bit with the courses. I had taken Data Structures and Algorithms and Probability and Simulation Based Inference because I had a background in these from my undergrad, but the Master's workload has been kinda brutal. So I studied the courses on [the MSDS Hub](https://msdshub.com/), which is an excellent resource during course planning, to try to figure out what the most amount of time I'm willing to spend outside of work. I tend to hit the `Scale of 1-10` button because it feels a bit more natural to me to gauge difficulty using that compared to `Scale of 1-5` or `Scale of 1-7`, sort on Workload, and eventually using that + the Program of Work, I decided on my next 2 oppressors, Foundations of Regression and Predictive Modelling  and Data Exploration and Visualization.

![Bounce back meme](/assets/images/memes/bounce-back.gif)

The interesting thing about this semester is that I decided to do it from back home in Dubai, so my days for the next 4 months were completely flipped. I'd start work at 5ish pm, keep at it till 1/2am, go to the gym, sleep by like 5/6am, and then wake up whenever. Looking back, these days were not sustainable by any stretch and I have no idea how I was able to keep at it for so long. This schedule carried in into the weekends when I would start properly studying. I'd go to a cafe, order a simple tea, and get to watching lectures/completing assignments.

These 2 courses were very fun and, by far, the most practical courses I took throughout my degree. For starters, Dr. Wilke, who taught the Data Exploration and Visualization is a master speaker/course material preparer/data visualization guru. Not only is he one of the main developers responsible for maintaining `ggplot2`, the widely used R package that essentially defines how modern day data scientists do data visualization, but also he has authored "Fundamentals of Data Visualization" which he has in turn created a website for [here](https://clauswilke.com/dataviz/). There has genuinely never been a better suited professor to teach this course.

Now that I glazed the prof, I'll talk a bit about the course itself. The course itself was not fundamentally a challenging course. There were no complex formulas, no convoluted derivations, no underlying theory or science, instead this course was effectively teaching an art of how to present information in a digestible, clean, and effective way.

Throughout this course, I properly learned R and R markdown and finally learned how a data scientist could go about demonstrating their findings. I used to always think that scripts are the only way to go, maybe Jupyter Notebooks if you're really fancy, but there's something more natural about using `ggplot2` that `matplotlib` could never achieve. It's also only now, 2 years later, that I find out that there's a Python package called [plotnine](https://plotnine.org/) that's trying to mimic what `ggplot2` does.

I'm more of a math-y, technical guy, but I really appreciated what this course taught. Just goes to show that good professors really make or break your experience. I used what I was learning in this course, namely the report writing and content rendering with R markdown skills, to help do the assignments for the Regression class.

Initially, I was confused as to why there was an entire 4 month semester class dedicated to just regression. Of course, I only thought a simple linear regression was all we cared about, but boy was I mistaken. This was the math heavy course that I liked. I found the professor, Dr. Stephen Walker, or at least his lectures to be a bit monotone and hard to follow along. I also found the sample code he used in his lectures to be a bit difficult to follow along with. You can't convince me that using variable names such as `xx` and `yy` which are supposedly "bootstrapped samples of x/y" as opposed to just the normal `x` and `y` variables is any way readable code. It's things like this that just give the student the impression that the professor just doesn't care enough to help them. Maybe I'm just not that smart.

Regardless, the course was excellent. Full of mathematical theory that I enjoy reading and following along with, and as of 2 years later, practical in my line of work, so I'm pretty happy.

I did get A's on both of these classes, so safe to say, I figured out what my studying time budget is in this semester.
